The United States of America (USA), commonly known as the United States (U.S.) or America, is a country primarily located in North America. It consists of 50 states, a federal district, five major unincorporated territories, and several minor islands.

It is the third-largest country by both land and total area. With a population of over 340 million, it is the third most populous country in the world. The national capital is Washington, D.C., and the most populous city and financial center is New York City.

Etymology

The first known use of the name "America" dates to 1507, when German cartographer Martin Waldseemüller produced a world map on which he named the lands of the Western Hemisphere "America" after the Italian explorer and cartographer Amerigo Vespucci.

History

Pre-Columbian and Colonial Period

Indigenous peoples have inhabited what is now the United States for thousands of years. Complex cultures developed, such as the Mississippian culture and the Ancestral Puebloans. European colonization began in the 16th century, with the Spanish in Florida and the Southwest, followed by the French along the Mississippi River, and the English on the East Coast.

Independence and Expansion (1776–1865)

1776 The Thirteen Colonies declare independence from Great Britain, initiating the American Revolutionary War.
1787 The Constitution of the United States is adopted, establishing a strong federal government.
1803 The Louisiana Purchase doubles the size of the nation.
1861–1865 The American Civil War is fought between the northern Union states and the southern Confederate states, resulting in the abolition of slavery.
Union soldiers during the Civil War.

Industrialization and Global Power (1865–1945)

Following the Civil War, the United States underwent rapid industrialization. The completion of the Transcontinental Railroad in 1869 connected the East and West coasts. By the early 20th century, the U.S. had become the world's leading industrial power.

The U.S. entered World War I in 1917. After the war, the "Roaring Twenties" ended with the Great Depression. The U.S. entered World War II after the attack on Pearl Harbor in 1941, emerging from the conflict as a global superpower equipped with nuclear weapons.

Cold War and Modern Era (1945–Present)

The post-war era was defined by the Cold War geopolitical tension with the Soviet Union and the Space Race. In 1969, the Apollo 11 mission landed the first humans on the Moon.

Buzz Aldrin on the lunar surface, 1969.

The Civil Rights Movement of the 1950s and 60s led to legislation banning racial discrimination. Following the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, the U.S. became the world's sole superpower. The 21st century began with the September 11 attacks, leading to the War on Terror.

Geography and Climate

The contiguous United States is bordered by Canada to the north and Mexico to the south. The country contains a vast array of landscapes, from the humid East Coast to the arid Southwest, the Rocky Mountains, and the temperate Pacific Northwest. The climate varies from tropical in Hawaii and Florida to subarctic in Alaska.

Government and Politics

The United States is a federal republic and a representative democracy. The government is regulated by a system of checks and balances defined by the U.S. Constitution, which serves as the country's supreme legal document.

Economy

The U.S. economy is a highly developed mixed economy. It is the world's largest economy by nominal GDP and the second-largest by purchasing power parity (PPP). The U.S. dollar is the currency most used in international transactions and is the world's primary reserve currency.

The nation is a global hub for technology and innovation, particularly in Silicon Valley, and possesses the world's largest stock exchanges, the New York Stock Exchange and Nasdaq.
